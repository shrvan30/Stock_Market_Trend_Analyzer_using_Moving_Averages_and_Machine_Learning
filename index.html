Learn About Candlestick Patterns

Hammer

A hammer is a price pattern in candlestick charting that occurs when a security trades significantly lower than its opening, but rallies within the period to close near the opening price.

Shooting Star

The shooting star pattern emerges on candlestick charts, which visually represent price movements over a specific period.

Engulfing

Indicates a strong reversal with larger candles engulfing smaller ones.

Doji

Represents market indecision; can signal reversal or continuation.

What is a Moving Average?

A moving average (MA) smooths out price data to identify trends. It helps traders see the overall market direction without short-term price fluctuations.

Formula for Standard RSI:
RSI = 100 − [100 / (1 + RS)]
Where:
RS (Relative Strength) = Average Gain / Average Loss over the selected period (usually 14).

Types of Moving Average

1. Crossover Signals
Crossovers occur when a shorter-term moving average crosses above or below a longer-term moving average. These events can be important indicators of changing market sentiment.

a) Bullish Crossover (Golden Cross)
Signal: This occurs when a shorter-term moving average crosses above a longer-term moving average.
Examples:
- 100-day moving average crossing above the 200-day moving average
- 100-day moving average crossing above the 250-day moving average
Interpretation: A bullish crossover is considered a positive signal, often indicating the start of an uptrend.
Type: Bullish

b) Bearish Crossover (Death Cross)
Signal: This happens when a shorter-term moving average crosses below a longer-term moving average.
Examples:
- 100-day moving average crossing below the 200-day moving average
- 100-day moving average crossing below the 250-day moving average
Interpretation: A bearish crossover is viewed as a negative signal, often suggesting that a downtrend may be starting.
Type: Bearish

c) 200-day and 250-day Moving Average Crossover
Signal: A crossover between the 200-day and 250-day moving averages is rarer, but it can still indicate a long-term trend shift.
Interpretation: If the 200-day moving average crosses above the 250-day moving average, it suggests increasing bullish momentum. Conversely, if the 200-day crosses below the 250-day, it indicates weakening long-term price trends and the possibility of a downtrend.
Type: Long-term Trend Shift

2. Support and Resistance Signals
Moving averages, especially the 200-day and 250-day, often serve as dynamic support and resistance levels.

a) Price Bouncing off Moving Averages (Support or Resistance)
Signal: If the price is above a moving average and then pulls back to it and bounces higher, this moving average is acting as support. Similarly, if the price is below a moving average and bounces lower after approaching it, the moving average acts as resistance.
Interpretation: Moving averages, especially the 200-day and 250-day, often act as long-term support or resistance.

b) Price Breaking Through Moving Averages
Signal: When the price crosses through a moving average, it is a significant signal. For example, if the price breaks through the 200-day moving average to the downside, it suggests a potential trend reversal or weakening of the current uptrend.
Interpretation: A breakout above a moving average is a sign of bullish momentum, while a breakdown below a moving average signals bearish momentum.

3. Trend Confirmation Signals
The alignment of multiple moving averages can help confirm a trend.

a) Bullish Trend Confirmation
Signal: If the 100-day moving average is above the 200-day moving average and the 200-day is above the 250-day moving average, this alignment suggests a strong uptrend.
Interpretation: This indicates that both short-term and long-term trends are pointing upward, providing strong confirmation that the market is in a bullish phase.

b) Bearish Trend Confirmation
Signal: If the 100-day moving average is below the 200-day moving average, and the 200-day is below the 250-day moving average, this alignment indicates a strong downtrend.
Interpretation: This shows that both short-term and long-term trends are pointing downward, signaling that the market is in a bearish phase.

4. Divergence and Convergence Signals
Divergence and convergence between price action and moving averages can give insights into potential reversals or continuation of trends.

a) Positive Divergence
Signal: Positive divergence occurs when the price forms lower lows, but the moving average (such as the 100-day or 200-day) forms higher lows.
Interpretation: This suggests that selling pressure is weakening, and a reversal to the upside could be imminent. It’s a potential signal of a bullish reversal.

b) Negative Divergence
Signal: Negative divergence occurs when the price forms higher highs, but the moving average forms lower highs.
Interpretation: This suggests that upward momentum is slowing down, and a price reversal to the downside could be coming. It’s a potential signal of a bearish reversal.

5. Moving Average "Flatness" or "Slope"
The slope and direction of moving averages give an indication of the trend’s strength.

a) Flat Moving Averages
Signal: If the moving averages (especially the 100-day, 200-day, or 250-day) become flat, it suggests a lack of trend, which can signal market indecision or consolidation.
Interpretation: A flat moving average implies that the market is in a period of range-bound trading and may not have a clear direction.

b) Steeply Rising or Falling Moving Averages
Signal: If the moving averages are sloping steeply upwards (bullish) or downwards (bearish), this indicates a strong trend.
Interpretation: A steep upward slope of a moving average signals strong bullish momentum, while a steep downward slope signals strong bearish momentum.

6. Crossovers with Volume
Volume can be used to confirm moving average crossovers.

a) Volume Increase on Bullish Crossover
Signal: If a bullish crossover (e.g., the 100-day crossing above the 200-day) occurs alongside a significant increase in volume, it indicates strong market conviction behind the uptrend.
Interpretation: High volume confirms that the crossover is supported by strong market participation, making it a more reliable signal.

b) Volume Increase on Bearish Crossover
Signal: A bearish crossover (e.g., the 100-day crossing below the 200-day) with high volume indicates strong conviction behind the downtrend.
Interpretation: The increased volume confirms that the market is likely to continue its downward movement.

What is RSI?

The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and change of price movements. It helps traders identify overbought (above 70) and oversold (below 30) conditions.

Types of RSI

1) Standard RSI (14-period)
Most commonly used RSI with a 14-period calculation. Good for identifying general trends but may lag in fast-moving markets.

2) Stochastic RSI (StochRSI)
Applies the stochastic oscillator formula to RSI values instead of price data. More sensitive to price changes, making it useful for short-term traders.
Formula: StochRSI = [RSI − Lowest(RSI, n)] / [Highest(RSI, n) − Lowest(RSI, n)]. Ranges between 0 and 1 instead of 0-100.

3) Connors RSI (CRSI)
A combination of 3 indicators: Standard RSI (short-term, like 3-period RSI), Streak Length (counts consecutive up/down closes), and Percentage Rank (compares today's price change with previous changes). Helps detect short-term reversals more effectively.

4) Cutler’s RSI
Similar to standard RSI but uses Simple Moving Average (SMA) instead of Exponential Moving Average (EMA). Results in slightly different values, but overall, it behaves similarly.

5) Dynamic RSI
The RSI period adjusts automatically based on market volatility. When the market is volatile, RSI uses a shorter period for quick reactions. When the market is calm, it uses a longer period to reduce noise.
